# Larman's Laws of Organizational Behavior

Craig Larman's five observational laws, distilled from decades of consulting in large organizations. Source: [craiglarman.com](https://www.craiglarman.com/wiki/index.php?title=Larman%27s_Laws_of_Organizational_Behavior)

---

**Law 1:** "Organizations are implicitly optimized to avoid changing the status quo middle- and first-level manager and 'specialist' positions & power structures."

**Law 2:** "As a corollary to (1), any change initiative will be reduced to redefining or overloading the new terminology to mean basically the same as status quo."

**Law 3:** "As a corollary to (1), any change initiative will be derided as 'purist', 'theoretical', 'revolutionary', 'religion', and 'needing pragmatic customization for local concerns' — which deflects from addressing weaknesses and manager/specialist status quo."

**Law 4:** "As a corollary to (1), if after _changing the change_ some managers and single-specialists are still displaced, they become 'coaches/trainers' for the change, frequently reinforcing (2) and (3), and creating the false impression 'the change has been done', deluding senior management and future change attempts, after which they become industry consultants."

**Law 5:** "(in large established orgs) _Culture follows structure_. And in tiny young orgs, structure follows culture."

---

In large organizations, culture/behavior/mindset follows and is influenced by changes in the organizational system and design — teams, hierarchies, roles, reward mechanisms. Mindset-only change initiatives lack staying power. Systems thinker John Seddon: *"Peoples' behavior...is a product of the system; when you change the system peoples' behavior changes."*

Frameworks like Scrum drive cultural impact through structural changes, not exhortations.
